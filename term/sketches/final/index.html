Data Visualization Proposals

Student: Sujan Pokhrel

Dataset: Personal Screen Time, Stress, Sleep, and Productivity Tracking

Dataset Description

I have been collecting daily data on four interconnected aspects of my life: screen time, stress levels, sleep duration, and productivity ratings. This data spans 14 days and captures:

  • Screen Time: Total daily screen exposure in hours, including phone, computer, and tablet usage (range: 4.8-9.2 hours)
  • Stress Level: Self-assessed on a 1-5 scale, where 1 represents minimal stress and 5 represents very high stress (range: 1-5)
  • Sleep Hours: Total sleep duration per night in hours, measured from bedtime to wake time (range: 5.5-8.5 hours)
  • Productivity: Self-rated daily productivity on a 1-5 scale, where 1 represents very unproductive and 5 represents highly productive (range: 1-5)

Sketch 1: Enhanced Scatterplot with Sleep Bubbles

Hypothesis:

"As daily screen time increases, stress levels increase. Additionally, decreased sleep hours correlate with both higher stress and lower productivity levels."

Data Elements Used:

  • X-axis: Screen Time (hours) - Range: 4-10 hours
  • Y-axis: Stress Level (1-5 scale) - Range: 0-6 (extended to show full context)
  • Circle Size: Sleep Hours - Range: 5.5-8.5 hours (larger circles = more sleep)
  • Circle Color: Productivity Level (1-5 scale) - Gradient from light green (#a8e6cf) representing low productivity to dark green (#004d40) representing high productivity

Visual Representation:

This bubble plot positions each day as a circle where the horizontal position shows screen time and the vertical position shows stress level. The size of each bubble represents sleep duration, with larger bubbles indicating more sleep hours. The color encodes productivity, with darker green shades representing higher productivity levels. This design allows viewers to see four dimensions simultaneously and identify patterns such as "high screen time combined with low sleep results in high stress and low productivity." The visualization uses proportional sizing (square root scale) to ensure accurate perception of sleep differences, and semi-transparent circles with black outlines prevent larger bubbles from completely obscuring smaller ones.

Sketch 2: Parallel Coordinates Plot

Question:

"What are the multi-dimensional patterns between screen time, stress, sleep, and productivity, and can we identify 'healthy' versus 'unhealthy' day profiles based on how these variables cluster together?"

Data Elements Used:

  • Axis 1: Screen Time (hours) - Range: 4-10 hours, displayed on vertical scale
  • Axis 2: Stress Level (1-5 scale) - Range: 1-5, displayed on vertical scale
  • Axis 3: Sleep Hours - Range: 5-9 hours, displayed on vertical scale
  • Axis 4: Productivity Level (1-5 scale) - Range: 1-5, displayed on vertical scale
  • Line Color: Stress level - Gradient from blue (#2196f3) for low stress (1-2), through orange (#ffa726) for moderate stress (3), to red (#f44336) for high stress (4-5)

Visual Representation:

Four vertical axes are arranged side-by-side at equal intervals, each representing one variable scaled from its minimum to maximum value. Each day in the dataset is represented by a colored line that connects its values across all four axes. Lines are colored based on stress level, making it immediately visible which days were calm (blue) versus stressful (red). This visualization excels at revealing whether certain combinations of values consistently occur together. For example, blue lines (low stress days) tend to pass through high values on the sleep axis and low values on the screen time axis, while also ending high on the productivity axis. This clustering pattern makes it easy to identify "healthy day profiles" versus "unhealthy day profiles" and understand the relationships between all four dimensions simultaneously.

Sketch 3: Radial Sleep-Stress Flower Chart

Hypothesis:

"Days with optimal sleep (7.5-8.5 hours) cluster toward lower stress and higher productivity, creating a visual 'bloom' pattern when stress is low. As screen time increases, this pattern 'wilts' toward higher stress regions, with petals appearing smaller and closer to the center."

Data Elements Used:

  • Angular Position: Screen Time (hours) - Range: 4-10 hours, mapped to 0-360 degrees around the circle (like hours on a clock face)
  • Radial Distance from Center: Sleep Hours - Range: 5-9 hours, where points closer to the center represent less sleep and points further out represent more sleep
  • Petal Color: Stress Level (1-5 scale) - Gradient from calm blue (#2196f3) for low stress to intense red (#f44336) for high stress
  • Petal Shape/Size: Productivity Level (1-5 scale) - Larger, fuller petal shapes represent higher productivity (level 4-5), medium shapes represent moderate productivity (level 3), and smaller petals represent lower productivity (level 1-2)

Visual Representation:

This creative visualization arranges data in a circular "flower" pattern where each day appears as a petal extending from the center. The center of the circle represents minimal sleep, and petals extend outward based on sleep hours—the more sleep, the further from center. Each petal's angular position around the circle (0-360 degrees) corresponds to screen time hours, similar to hours on a clock. Petal color encodes stress level using an intuitive blue-to-red scale (blue for calm, red for stressed), while petal size represents productivity level. "Healthy days" appear as large blue petals in the outer ring, representing lots of sleep, low stress, and high productivity— these petals "bloom" outward. "Unhealthy days" appear as small red petals near the center, representing poor sleep, high stress, and low productivity—these petals "wilt" inward. This metaphorical approach makes the data emotionally resonant and immediately understandable while remaining quantitatively accurate. Concentric guide rings mark different sleep hour zones, and angular markers indicate screen time ranges, ensuring precise data reading despite the creative format.

Design Resources & Citations

1. Data to Viz Decision Tree

Source: https://www.data-to-viz.com/

How I used it: I used the interactive decision tree on this site to determine which visualization types work best with my dataset of four numeric variables. Starting from the "Explore" tab, I selected my data type (numeric for all variables) and the tool suggested several appropriate formats including scatterplots and parallel coordinates. This directly influenced my choice of parallel coordinates for Sketch 2, as the site explained that this format excels at showing patterns and clusters across multiple continuous variables simultaneously. The examples showed me how lines connecting values across axes can reveal "profiles" in the data, which aligned perfectly with my goal of identifying healthy versus unhealthy day patterns.

2. Observable D3 Gallery - Parallel Coordinates

Source: https://observablehq.com/@d3/parallel-coordinates

How I used it: This interactive example demonstrated the technical implementation of parallel coordinates plots, showing me exactly how to arrange multiple vertical axes at equal intervals and connect data points with lines. The example used monochrome lines, but it inspired me to add stress-based color encoding to make patterns more immediately visible in my Sketch 2. I also learned from this example that semi-transparent lines are essential when many lines overlap, as it allows viewers to see density patterns where multiple days share similar profiles. The axis labeling approach in this example informed my decision to clearly label each axis at both top and bottom with variable names and ranges.

3. Dear Data Project

Source: http://www.dear-data.com/theproject

How I used it: The Dear Data project's organic, hand-drawn visualizations inspired my Sketch 3 radial flower design. Specifically, I studied several weeks where the creators used radial and circular layouts to represent time-based personal data. Week 3's "A Week of Complaints" used radial positioning to encode different aspects of data, which helped me conceptualize how to map my four variables onto a circular structure. The project taught me that creative, metaphorical visualizations can be both emotionally engaging and quantitatively accurate when designed with clear encoding rules. This inspired me to use the "blooming flower" metaphor where healthy days bloom outward and unhealthy days wilt inward, making the data more intuitive and memorable while maintaining precise numerical relationships through radial distance and angular position.

4. "Visualizing Data: Mapping Data onto Aesthetics" (Fundamentals of Data Visualization)

Source: O'Reilly Learning Platform - Fundamentals of Data Visualization, Chapter 2

Access: Via O'Reilly temporary access

How I used it: This chapter's detailed explanation of the "rule of proportional ink" was crucial for my Sketch 1 design. The reading explained that when encoding data with area (such as circle size), the visual element's area should be proportional to the data value, not the radius. This taught me to use a square root scale (d3.scaleSqrt) for my bubble sizes rather than a linear scale, ensuring that a day with 8 hours of sleep appears to have twice the circle area (not twice the radius) of a day with 4 hours of sleep. Without this reading, my bubble plot would have contained visual distortions that could mislead viewers about the magnitude of sleep differences. The chapter also reinforced the importance of using appropriate color scales, which influenced my choice of a single-hue gradient (light to dark green) for productivity in Sketch 1 rather than a multi-hue rainbow that might imply ordering where none exists.

5. Information Is Beautiful - NYT Best Visualizations

Source: https://www.informationisbeautifulawards.com/

How I used it: Studying award-winning visualizations from The New York Times and other publications taught me that clarity and immediate comprehension should take priority over visual complexity or "coolness factor." Many winning designs used simple, clear visual encodings with comprehensive legends rather than elaborate graphics. This influenced several decisions across all three sketches: keeping my color schemes simple and intuitive (blue for calm, red for stress), ensuring all axes have clear labels with units, and including detailed legends that explain every visual encoding. The examples also showed me the importance of annotations and callouts that guide the viewer's attention to key patterns, which I incorporated into all three sketches with text labels like "High screen time → higher stress" and "Healthy days bloom outward."

6. The Data Visualisation Catalogue

Source: https://datavizcatalogue.com/

How I used it: I used the "Search by Function" page to explore visualization types suitable for showing correlations and comparisons across multiple variables. This catalogue provided clear explanations of when to use each chart type and what kinds of insights each reveals. It helped me confirm that scatterplots are ideal for showing correlations between two continuous variables (Sketch 1) and that adding size and color encodings can extend this to four dimensions. The catalogue's description of radial visualizations also validated my creative approach in Sketch 3, showing that circular layouts can effectively represent cyclical or related data when designed with clear positional encodings.
